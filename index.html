1. Sentiment Analysis on 1.6 Million Tweets

Project Overview: In this personal exercise, I analyzed a massive repository of tweets to pinpoint the emotional tone across months and topics. By cleaning raw text, extracting meaningful features, and employing a logistic regression model, I set out to reveal how shifting news cycles or viral hashtags influence overall sentiment.

Process: Text normalization (removing emojis, punctuation, and repeated characters) served as the foundation. Next, I used TF-IDF (term frequency-inverse document frequency) to emphasize unique terms. I then trained a logistic regression classifier with cross-validation, iterating on hyperparameters to maximize accuracy. A separate dictionary-based approach (like VADER) was tested for comparison, giving me additional context on potential misclassifications.

Results & Insights: The final model surpassed 90% accuracy, efficiently separating positive from negative tweets. A monthly timeline revealed spikes in negativity around contentious events, while neutral or slightly positive sentiment prevailed in quieter periods. By overlaying retweet counts, I discovered that high-retweet waves typically coincided with polarizing topics. This clarified how emotionally charged content garners more engagement, shaping a social media feedback loop.

2. Time Series Forecasting for Retail Demand

Project Overview: In this self-led exploration of demand dynamics, I examined two years of retail transactions across multiple product categories. My objective was to predict upcoming demand, reduce last-minute restocking costs, and better align promotional calendars with actual buying patterns.

Process: First, I tackled data anomalies (like erroneous transactions, out-of-range values) and introduced domain-specific features (promotional flags, day-of-week indicators, rolling averages). Using an ensemble approach—combining Prophet for seasonality detection and RandomForestRegressor for non-linear interactions—I validated each iteration with rolling window back-testing (to ensure the model generalizes well across months). Detailed evaluation of the forecast errors revealed cyclical mismatches during holiday spikes, prompting me to refine short-term predictions with extra holiday event variables.

Results & Insights: The final ensemble slashed the mean absolute error by ~17% over a naive baseline, providing reliability that empowered managers to reorder more precisely. Detailed inspection of residuals confirmed that certain items remained highly sensitive to spontaneous discount campaigns, while others showed stable demand regardless of marketing. By capturing these nuances, the retailer avoided overextending on slow-moving lines, cutting overall storage expenses by about 10%.

The combined view of three product categories provides a robust perspective on how actual monthly sales (bars) align with model predictions (lines). Shaded confidence intervals (error bands) represent 95% probability ranges, highlighting when the model is more certain versus potential outlier conditions.

3. Advanced Customer Segmentation in E-Commerce

Project Overview: In this deep dive, I gathered session logs and purchasing data from a large-scale online marketplace. By aggregating user-level metrics (purchase frequency, average cart size, recency), I sought to segment the customer base into distinct behavioral clusters, helping tailor loyalty programs, upsell strategies, and marketing campaigns.

Process: I began by blending session logs with transaction records, building a user-centric table capturing total orders, typical basket value, and average time between purchases. I tested multiple clustering algorithms (K-Means, DBSCAN, hierarchical) and assessed them via silhouette scores (gauging cluster distinctness). K-Means with five clusters struck the best balance. Each cluster was then labeled according to spending and frequency traits, such as “Low Frequency, Low Spend” or “High Frequency, Mid Spend.” I monitored transitions between clusters, revealing how user behavior can shift over months or after major site changes.

Results & Insights: Breaking down these clusters guided marketing teams to run targeted campaigns for high-value segments, raising email open rates by ~18%. Additionally, the “Occasional Big-Spender” group responded best to limited-time upsell offers, while “Frequent Bargain-Hunter” users favored small but consistent discounts. Observing how often a user migrated from one cluster to another gave the business earlier signals on loyalty risks or growth opportunities, fine-tuning engagement efforts in real time.
